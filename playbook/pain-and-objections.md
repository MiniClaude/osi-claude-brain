# Pain Points, Discovery Questions, and Objection Handling

Read this when writing a Pain-Led email, a live call script, or any first touch that leads with the prospect's problem. Lead with pain, not product. The product is the answer to a pain they already feel.

Apply `voice-rules.md` to anything that goes into an email or a spoken script: no em-dashes, no hyphens in outbound copy, peer to peer tone.

## The spine: secondary source, not rip and replace

The lowest risk way in is as a second option, not a replacement. We are not asking anyone to fire their incumbent on the first call. We are the faster, lower cost source they call when timing or budget bites. This reframe sidesteps the "we already have a vendor" wall and drops the buyer's risk to near zero.

Core line: "I'm not asking you to replace anyone. Most of our best relationships started as a second opinion on one project."

Pricing discipline: lead with the pain and the value. Let them ask about cost, then give the number. Never open with "we're cheaper." The angle is risk, time, and fit, not price.

## Pain points by product line

Each pain has a discovery question that surfaces it. Use the question on a call, or as the one line ask in a Pain-Led Email 1.

### Optics (network engineer, architect)
- **Pain:** the break glass scenario. Something fails and you cannot wait two weeks for an OEM optic. No Plan B on the shelf.
- **Pain:** paying the OEM logo tax. A branded optic and the equivalent do the same job, one costs far more.
- **Discovery:** "What's your current source for optics when you have a lead time issue or something fails unexpectedly?"

### DWDM (transport engineer, optical NE, network planner)
- **Pain:** capacity constrained on an existing DWDM system, and the only option on the table is another expensive OEM capacity add with long lead times and licensing.
- **Discovery:** "Have you looked at open line DWDM as an alternative to Ciena or Nokia for your next capacity add?"

### TPM (IT director, DC manager, asset manager, procurement)
- **Pain:** carrying OEM support cost on gear that has been running fine for years. Renewal pressure on SmartNet. After the Park Place and Service Express merger, rate creep and a support model shifting toward margins.
- **Discovery:** "Are you on an OEM support contract for your Cisco gear, or have you looked at third party maintenance?"
- **Discovery:** "Is your TPM provider still Park Place or Service Express, or did you move after the merger?"

### Compute and DIMMs (systems, infrastructure, server admin)
- **Pain:** DDR5 pricing spiking, paying OEM channel markup on memory, buying RAM regularly with no direct source.
- **Discovery:** "When your DDR4 or DDR5 memory needs come up, are you going direct to Dell or HPE, or do you have another source?"

### Storage (storage admin, storage engineer)
- **Pain:** expensive storage refresh, long lead times on new gear, OEM support cost on NetApp running fine.
- **Discovery:** "If you're looking at a storage refresh or trying to extend the life of existing gear, who are you sourcing through today?"

### Refresh timing (any infrastructure owner)
- **Pain:** stuck mid cycle, or facing a refresh decision with a tighter budget than last cycle.
- **Discovery:** "What does your hardware refresh cycle look like right now, are you mid cycle or coming up on a decision?"

## Objection handlers

Reframes, not rebuttals. Each one stays peer to peer and gives the prospect an easy out. Pick the pain point that fits them.

- **"We already have a vendor for that."**
  "Totally understand, most of our best customers have a primary vendor. We work as a second source for faster lead times and lower cost, no disruption to what's already working. Worth keeping a number on file for the next time timing or cost becomes an issue?"

- **"We're under contract."**
  "Got it. When does that renew? I would rather be in your ear three months before than three days after."

- **"Not interested."**
  "Fair enough. Before I let you go, is it timing, or is it just not on the roadmap at all? I would rather not bug you if it's the latter."

- **"Too busy right now."**
  "I'll be quick. One question: is [their pain point] something that's come up in the last six months? If yes, worth fifteen minutes. If no, I'll leave you alone."

- **"Send me an email."**
  "Happy to. What's the most relevant piece for where you are right now? I would rather send you one useful thing than a brochure." If you already emailed: "Already did, the subject was [subject]. I'll make sure it didn't land in spam. Is [address] still the best place to reach you?"

## Live call opener variants (beyond the openers in opener-library.md)

- **Honesty led pattern interrupt:** "[Name], this is Brian with OSI Global. I'll be straight, this is a cold call. Give me sixty seconds to tell you why I called, then tell me to get lost or keep going. Fair?"
- **Low pressure close:** "I'm not trying to run a full pitch on a cold call. Would it make sense to find twenty minutes to compare notes? Worst case you get a second opinion, best case we find something worth looking at."
